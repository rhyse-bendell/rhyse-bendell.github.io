## Project Overview

This project examined how AI-enabled tools can support military planning and scalable cross-echelon command and control in complex operational environments. Conducted in collaboration with the U.S. Army Research Laboratory, the work focused on how human planners interact with emerging decision-support technologies intended to accelerate course-of-action (COA) development, support iterative refinement, and improve the assessment of operational options. Rather than treating AI as a replacement for human planning, the project was grounded in the idea that useful systems must fit within real command workflows, support judgment under uncertainty, and strengthen collaborative reasoning across echelons.

At a broader research level, the project sat at the intersection of command and control, team cognition, and human-machine teaming. A central concern was how military planning teams maintain unity of effort when operating with time pressure, distributed responsibilities, evolving information, and increasingly sophisticated digital tools. This made the project as much about cognition and coordination as it was about interface design: we were interested in how AI support changes planning speed, reasoning processes, trust, burden, and the ability to evaluate and revise plans effectively.

The effort included literature reviews, technical reporting, theory translation, and empirical study development, but one of its most visible components was the evaluation of an Integrated Technology System (ITS) that included COA-GPT and the SC3 simulator/interface. COA-GPT was designed to generate and refine seed COAs from planning materials and user input, while the simulator and interface allowed users to inspect those plans, make structured changes, and assess projected outcomes. Together, these tools were intended to support more rapid, better-structured, and more testable planning workflows than current manual methods alone.

My role in this project was substantial. I was heavily involved in study design, helped direct and conduct the data collection, supported the semi-structured interview process with subject matter experts, and contributed to the interpretation and reporting of the findings. I also produced most of the Soldier Touchpoint report that synthesized design feedback, research implications, and recommendations for future experimentation and system development.

## Methods and Outcomes

To evaluate the technologies in a realistic but developmental setting, we conducted a structured touchpoint study with experienced military subject matter experts. The study was designed to gather detailed feedback on usability, trust, planning value, and future development priorities for the combined system. SMEs interacted with the ITS across multiple sessions that included individual work with seed COAs, collaborative planning around a shared best COA, and direct refinement of plans through COA-GPT. These sessions were supported by surveys, observational notes, and semi-structured feedback designed to capture both immediate usability issues and broader implications for future command-and-control research.

Methodologically, the project combined literature-informed measure development with applied human-in-the-loop evaluation. The larger research plan included identifying team cognitive functions relevant to military planning, developing measures for those processes, conducting SME interviews, and using simulation-based studies to understand how AI affects planning, coordination, and decision support across echelons. Within the touchpoint itself, the emphasis was on evaluating how the system supported mission analysis, seed COA generation, plan editing, simulation-based assessment, and collaborative review under realistic planning expectations.

The overall outcome was encouraging. SMEs viewed the technology as promising and directionally effective, even in an early and partially integrated state. In particular, they saw substantial value in the ability to start from an AI-generated COA, refine it through structured edits, and inspect projected outcomes through simulation. The system was not treated as final or operationally complete, but it was seen as a meaningful improvement over starting from a blank slate and as a strong foundation for future refinement. Feedback consistently suggested that the integrated system could reduce planning time and cognitive effort, especially for individual planners, and that a more mature version could become highly valuable for team-based planning environments.

At the same time, the study clarified where further development would matter most. SMEs emphasized the importance of better temporal phasing, improved traceability from generated recommendations back to source materials, tighter synchronization between COA-GPT and the simulator, and stronger support for collaborative and distributed planning roles. Even with those limitations, the technologies were regarded as effective enough to justify continued development and rigorous future experimentation. From a portfolio perspective, this project demonstrates work not only on AI-enabled military planning tools, but on the careful empirical evaluation needed to make those tools credible, usable, and relevant to real-world command workflows.
