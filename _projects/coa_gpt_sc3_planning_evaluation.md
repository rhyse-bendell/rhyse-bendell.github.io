## Evaluation Overview
In collaboration with Army Research Lab partners and Mission Command Battle Lab stakeholders, this project focused on evaluating an emerging AI-supported planning stack for military course-of-action (COA) development and refinement. The effort was designed as an applied human-systems research study: not just whether the tools worked in isolation, but how they changed planning workflow quality, tempo, and cognitive burden when experts used them in realistic decision-making contexts.

## Planning Stack Under Study
The touchpoint evaluated an Integrated Technology System (ITS) assembled by the broader SC3 effort. In this study context, the stack combined:

- **COA-GPT** to generate and revise seed COAs from mission materials and planner inputs.
- **SC3 Simulator and interface** to inspect likely outcomes, test alternatives, and support iterative refinement.

My role was centered on evaluating this integrated stack with SMEs; it was not a software-authorship role for the underlying COA-GPT or simulator technologies.

## Research Leadership and Contribution
I helped shape and execute the SME evaluation strategy from end to end: defining the study framing, structuring the task flow, and selecting measures that captured planning effectiveness beyond simple speed metrics. The evaluation emphasized planner cognition and workflow realities, including trust calibration, usability, coordination demands, and workload distribution across planning steps.

I also synthesized the qualitative and quantitative findings into a substantial technical report that translated expert feedback into actionable design and research priorities for the next stage of system maturation.

## What We Examined
The study focused on practical planning questions that matter in operational teams:

- Whether AI-supported COA generation gave planners a better starting point than beginning from a blank slate.
- How simulation-supported iteration changed the speed and clarity of evaluating alternatives.
- Which usability and trust patterns supported effective human judgment, versus where friction or uncertainty emerged.
- What requirements had to be met for stronger distributed team-planning support.

## Findings and Design Implications
SMEs viewed the early integrated stack as promising, especially for generating initial options, supporting structured edits, and enabling rapid simulation-assisted iteration. Across the touchpoint, experts estimated meaningful efficiency benefits as capabilities mature: roughly **30%** reductions in COA-development time and cognitive effort with fuller integration, with larger potential gains (on the order of **60-70%**) in a more mature collaborative end state.

At the same time, the evaluation identified clear design conditions for trustworthy team use at scale: stronger temporal phasing and sequencing support, better terrain and doctrinal grounding, improved traceability of AI-supported plan changes, and more robust distributed collaboration features.

## Why This Project Matters
This project represents the kind of applied human-AI teaming work I aim to lead: evaluating emerging AI decision-support systems in high-stakes settings through rigorous study design, empirically grounded measurement, and workflow-aware analysis. It demonstrates an ability to turn messy expert feedback into concrete design direction while keeping both human performance and team coordination at the center of system development.
