## Project Overview

Generative artificial intelligence is rapidly transforming creative industries, raising fundamental questions about authorship, authenticity, and value. As AI systems become capable of producing visually compelling and stylistically sophisticated artwork, the boundary between human and machine creativity is becoming increasingly ambiguous. This project was designed to investigate how people perceive, evaluate, and emotionally respond to artwork when its origin—human or AI—is manipulated or misrepresented.

At its core, this research asks a deceptively simple question: Do people actually respond differently to AI-generated art, or do their reactions depend more on what they believe about its origin? By systematically examining perception, attribution, and evaluation, this work contributes to broader discussions in human–AI interaction, trust in automated systems, and the social construction of value in emerging technological contexts.

The project sits at the intersection of cognitive psychology, aesthetics, and human–AI teaming, providing empirical insight into how individuals interpret and assign meaning to outputs generated by artificial systems.

## Generative Art and Economic Impact

Generative AI tools—such as diffusion-based image models—are already reshaping creative labor markets. Artists, designers, and illustrators are encountering both new opportunities and direct competition from systems capable of producing high-quality visual content at scale and near-zero marginal cost. This has led to ongoing debates around intellectual property, compensation, and the role of human creativity in a rapidly automated pipeline.

Despite these economic and cultural disruptions, a growing body of research suggests that consumers are often unable to reliably distinguish between AI-generated and human-generated images. In many cases, evaluations of quality, aesthetics, and emotional impact are comparable across sources. This creates a critical tension:

- If people cannot consistently tell the difference,
- but still believe that human-created art is more valuable or meaningful,
- then perception may be driven more by attribution than by the artifact itself.

Gener-AI-te was designed to directly interrogate this tension by separating what participants see from what they are told about what they see.

## The Gener-AI-te Study

The Gener-AI-te study used a controlled experimental design to examine how art origin (human vs. AI) and labeling (accurate, absent, or deceptive) influence participant judgments.

Participants were recruited through online platforms, including university subject pools and art-related communities, and completed the study via Qualtrics. The procedure consisted of two main phases:

### 1. Individual Differences and Baseline Measures

Participants first completed a series of validated questionnaires capturing:

- Personality traits (e.g., Big Five)
- Art knowledge and familiarity
- Art training and education
- Style preferences
- Expectations about art experiences
- General attitudes toward AI
- Propensity to trust machines

These measures allowed us to model how pre-existing beliefs and traits shape responses to AI-generated content.

### 2. Image Evaluation Task

Participants then viewed a series of images, including both human-generated and AI-generated artworks. Critically, the study manipulated how these images were presented:

- Some images were labeled accurately (human or AI)
- Some were unlabeled
- Some were intentionally mislabeled (e.g., AI images labeled as human, and vice versa)

This deception was introduced to isolate the effect of perceived origin from actual origin.

For each image, participants reported:

- Whether they considered it “art” or not
- Liking and aesthetic evaluation
- Emotional response
- Overall perception of the piece

This design enabled direct comparison between:

- What the image is
- What participants believe it is
- How those beliefs shape evaluation

## Findings and Interpretation

Across studies, a consistent pattern emerged: perception of origin significantly influences evaluation, often more than the actual origin of the artwork itself.

Several key insights stand out:

- **Attribution effects are strong:** When participants believed an image was human-created, they often evaluated it more favorably—even when it was generated by AI.
- **AI bias is not purely perceptual:** Differences in evaluation are not reliably driven by visual features alone, but by beliefs, expectations, and attitudes toward AI.
- **Individual differences matter:** Factors such as trust in machines, familiarity with art, and prior attitudes toward AI meaningfully shape how participants interpret and evaluate images.
- **The “art” label is socially constructed:** Whether something is perceived as “art” is not solely a function of the object—it is influenced by contextual framing and perceived authorship.

Taken together, these findings suggest that the disruption caused by generative AI is not just technical or economic—it is fundamentally psychological and social. As AI systems become more integrated into creative domains, understanding how people assign value, meaning, and authorship becomes critical.

From a broader perspective, this work highlights a core principle relevant across human–AI systems:

**People do not evaluate AI outputs in a vacuum—they evaluate them through the lens of beliefs about the system that produced them.**
