## Project Overview

This project examined how people build, organize, and use mental maps when learning virtual environments. The broader research goal was to understand how different kinds of spatial knowledge are acquired and how those different knowledge types contribute to successful wayfinding. Rather than treating spatial cognition as a single undifferentiated ability, the research decomposed mental map formation into multiple related components: point knowledge about landmarks or target objects, route knowledge about how to travel between those points, point configuration knowledge about where those points are arranged in space, and route configuration knowledge about how routes interconnect into a larger navigable structure.

The project was designed to go beyond the common practice of measuring only overall navigation success. A central motivation was that wayfinding performance alone does not reveal what kind of mental representation a person has built, what information they relied on, or why one manipulation helped or hindered learning. To address that gap, I developed a controlled virtual-environment research program that paired custom-built simulations with a broad measurement battery intended to capture both the contents of participants’ mental maps and their ability to rely on those mental maps during navigation.

This work also used virtual environments not simply as a convenient stimulus format, but as a methodological tool for isolating processes that are difficult to study in the real world. Because the environments were custom built, it was possible to manipulate route complexity while holding landmark locations constant, alter whether participants learned by following hallways or moving directly through walls, and test whether intersection signage helped or hindered knowledge acquisition. That combination of simulation control and granular assessment made it possible to ask more specific questions about how mental maps are built and why some forms of environmental support do not always help in the way designers expect.

## Methods

The research consisted of two experimental studies that shared a common core design and procedure. Across both studies, participants learned a virtual environment under different training and environmental conditions and then completed a sequence of knowledge and navigation tests. Study 1 used a 2 × 2 between-subjects design crossing spatial exposure training modality with environment route complexity. Study 2 used a 2 × 2 between-subjects design crossing the same training manipulation with the presence or absence of route-intersection signage. Across the full dissertation sample, 265 undergraduate participants completed the research program.

A key part of the methodology was the training manipulation. In the point-training condition, participants traveled directly from one target object to another by passing through walls in an “as the crow flies” fashion. In the route-training condition, participants traveled through hallways, intersections, and doorways in a more natural route-following manner. This distinction allowed the studies to test whether point-related exposure and route-related exposure could be separated and whether participants could still reconstruct missing information about the environment even when their experience was intentionally constrained or made unnatural.

The virtual environments themselves were built in Unreal Engine and paired with a deliberately broad assessment system. Participants completed tests of point feature knowledge, point configuration knowledge through a pointing estimation task and a point-placement task, route knowledge through both route reproduction and route recognition, and route configuration knowledge through a sketched-map task. They then completed wayfinding tests in both the learned environment and a disrupted version of the environment in which route blockades forced rerouting. Performance was not treated as a single score; instead, it was quantified through multiple error and accuracy measures, along with wayfinding efficiency relative to the minimum possible completion time in each environment. In addition, the studies measured several individual-difference variables such as biological sex, sense of direction, spatial strategies, spatial orientation ability, and video game experience so that interpretation of the spatial-learning outcomes would not ignore meaningful variation across participants.

## Findings & Implications

The findings showed that mental map development is more resilient and more complicated than simple stage-based accounts might suggest. Participants did not acquire only one kind of spatial knowledge at a time, nor did route-following experience produce a clean advantage across all route-related outcomes. Instead, the studies suggested that people often acquire different forms of spatial knowledge in parallel and can sometimes reconstruct or infer missing spatial information even when their training experience is intentionally incomplete. One of the most striking results was that participants trained in the point-to-point condition, who never naturally traversed the hallways or intersections, were still often able to perform surprisingly well on route-related knowledge measures. This points to a robust and flexible spatial memory system rather than a fragile one.

At the environmental level, route complexity had the clearest and most reliable negative effect. Simpler environments supported better acquisition of some kinds of spatial knowledge, especially point configuration knowledge and route knowledge, than more complex environments did. By contrast, intersection signage produced more nuanced effects than expected. Rather than uniformly helping participants, signage sometimes appeared to increase cognitive load and interfere with point-feature learning, suggesting that adding cues to an environment does not automatically improve spatial cognition and may sometimes compete with the encoding of other information.

The most practically important finding was that different kinds of spatial knowledge contributed differently to wayfinding performance. Point-feature knowledge was the strongest and most consistent predictor of successful navigation, while route knowledge and configurational knowledge showed more conditional and interaction-based relationships with wayfinding. This matters because it shows that wayfinding success by itself is not a sufficient proxy for mental-map quality. A person may navigate effectively for different reasons, and two people with similar wayfinding outcomes may be relying on very different underlying knowledge structures.

Together, these findings matter for both theory and application. Theoretically, they support a more dynamic and parallel view of spatial learning and show that distinctions among point knowledge, route knowledge, point configuration knowledge, and route configuration knowledge are meaningful rather than redundant. Methodologically, the project demonstrates the value of using custom virtual environments to manipulate spatial experience in controlled ways that would be difficult or impossible in real built spaces. Practically, the research has implications for the design of training systems, virtual environments, navigation aids, and spatial-learning studies: if researchers or designers want to understand why navigation succeeds or fails, they need to measure the underlying contents of the mental map rather than relying on wayfinding time alone.
