## Project Overview
Artificial Social Intelligence for Successful Teams (ASIST) is a DARPA program focused on developing AI systems that function as effective teammates by modeling human cognition, inferring intent, and supporting coordination in dynamic, high-consequence settings. The core objective is to build artificial social intelligence: agent capabilities that interpret human goals, anticipate behavior, and contribute to collaborative problem solving rather than acting as isolated tools.

My work centered on the design, execution, and analysis of large-scale human-subject experiments evaluating AI-supported teams in complex simulated environments, including Minecraft-based urban search-and-rescue scenarios. Across these studies, I helped connect social and cognitive theory to measurable team behaviors and deployable AI support strategies.

## My Role
- Lead researcher and UCF team project manager
- Lead study designer for the final ASIST experiment
- Directed the UCF analysis pipeline across ASIST studies
- Developed the **flocking** method for quantifying team coordination
- Led cross-institution collaboration with IHMC, CMU, MIT, and partner teams
- Translated social and cognitive theory into testable AI hypotheses

## Technical Approach
- Human-subjects experimentation in simulated environments
- Minecraft-based urban search-and-rescue testbed
- Multimodal behavioral data collection (movement, communication, task events)
- Team cognition modeling (mental models and shared understanding)
- Intervention-based paradigms for AI teammate evaluation
- Spatial-temporal coordination analysis using the flocking framework

## Key Contributions / Outcomes
- Designed and executed large-scale studies of human–AI teaming performance
- Created new profiling methods for team cognition and coordination
- Contributed an analytic framework used by ASI agent developers
- Supported integration of UCF profiling methods into AI system development
- Produced datasets and preregistered studies used across the ASIST program
