## Project Overview

This project examines how transdisciplinary science teams actually do the work of science. Its central focus is not only the outcomes of collaboration, such as papers or proposals, but the epistemic and social processes through which researchers identify important problems, define a shared area of inquiry, integrate disciplinary perspectives, and develop research directions together. The work is especially concerned with context: how social setting, physical space, disciplinary background, and differing orientations to time shape the development of new scientific teams and the ideas they produce.

The project is organized around a series of workshops designed to bring together researchers from different backgrounds to collaborate on socio-ecological and resilience-related problems. These workshops function simultaneously as an intervention and a natural experiment. On one level, they are meant to help participants meet potential collaborators, generate integrative research ideas, and form teams capable of developing white papers, concept papers, and future grant proposals. On another, they allow us to observe how interdisciplinary teams emerge, how they negotiate differences in terminology and method, how they externalize and revise ideas, and what contextual factors appear to help or hinder successful collaboration.

Methodologically, the project combines workshop activities, surveys, observational protocols, collaborative diagramming, team-formation procedures, and follow-up assessments between events. Participants first engage in activities that expose them to one another’s expertise and collaboration styles, then move into more structured team-based synthesis tasks in which they map problems, define shared research questions, and begin developing joint work products. Throughout this process, the project captures both subjective and behavioral indicators of team formation, teamwork quality, interdisciplinary integration, and evolving shared understanding.

More broadly, this work is meant to contribute to the Science of Team Science by shifting attention toward the formative stages of scientific collaboration. Rather than studying established teams after they already exist, this project focuses on how new interdisciplinary teams come together, how context shapes that process, and how those early collaboration dynamics may influence future scientific productivity. In that sense, the project is about both transdisciplinary team formation and the deeper question of how scientific work itself becomes organized, externalized, and coordinated across people, spaces, and technologies.

## Ongoing Work

A major ongoing component of the project is the continued development of methods for observing team science as it unfolds across workshops and between them. This includes the refinement of surveys assessing collaboration readiness, teamwork quality, expertise coordination, and conflict management, as well as protocols for activities such as lightning talks, speed networking, individual relationship diagramming, and collaborative synthesis. Together, these methods are intended to capture how researchers identify promising collaborators, how teams form around shared interests, and how multidisciplinary ideas are translated into more coherent research directions over time.

Another major line of ongoing work centers on the measurement of “digital exhaust” from collaborative scientific activity. Much of contemporary team science occurs through shared digital systems such as collaborative documents, messaging tools, repositories, calendars, and project-management platforms. Our current work treats those technologies not only as infrastructure for teamwork, but also as embedded sensing systems that generate analyzable traces of participation, coordination, knowledge integration, and workflow rhythms. This makes it possible to move beyond one-time surveys or brief observations and toward a more continuous view of how scientific teams build shared artifacts, distribute work, and coordinate over time.

In practical terms, that means we are building toward an observational framework that links workshop behavior, team products, and digital collaboration traces into a more unified account of scientific teamwork. Shared documents, writing histories, revision patterns, communication exchanges, and artifact development can all provide evidence about who is contributing, how ideas are being integrated, where coordination bottlenecks arise, and whether collaboration is broadly distributed or concentrated in a few key individuals. This line of work is still developing, but it is a central part of the project’s long-term value because it opens the possibility of studying science teams in ways that are both theoretically grounded and embedded in their ordinary work practices.

Finally, the project remains active as a platform for understanding how context shapes the future of team science itself. The workshop model, the contextual measures, and the digital exhaust framework are all part of a broader effort to understand how scientific teams generate ideas, coordinate expertise, and prepare new research agendas in increasingly complex problem spaces. The immediate outputs may include collaborative papers and grant concepts, but the larger contribution is methodological and theoretical: building better ways to observe, explain, and eventually support the work of transdisciplinary science teams.
